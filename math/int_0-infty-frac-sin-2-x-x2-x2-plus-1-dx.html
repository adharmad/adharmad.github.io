Integrate \( \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin^2 x}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx \)

\[ I = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin^2 x}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx \] In order to use Feynman's method of differentiating under the sign of the integral, we need to parametrize the integral as: \[ I(a) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin^2 (ax)}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx \tag{1} \] Differentiating both sides w.r.t a, we get: \[ \frac{dI}{da} = \frac{d}{da} \; \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin^2 (ax)}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx = \int_0^\infty \frac{\partial}{\partial a} \frac{\sin^2 (ax)}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx \] \[ \frac{dI}{da} = \int_0^\infty \frac{2 \sin (ax) \cos (ax)}{x^2(x^2+1)} 2x \; dx = \int_0^\infty \frac{2 \sin (ax) \cos (ax)}{x(x^2+1)} \; dx \] \[ \frac{dI}{da} = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin (2ax)}{x(x^2+1)} \; dx \tag{2} \] Differentiating both sides w.r.t a again, we get: \[ \frac{d^2I}{da^2} = \frac{d}{da} \; \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin (2ax)}{x(x^2+1)} \; dx = \int_0^\infty \frac{\partial}{\partial a} \frac{\sin (2ax)}{x(x^2+1)} \; dx \] \[ \frac{d^2I}{da^2} = \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos (2ax)}{x(x^2+1)} 2x \; dx \] \[ \frac{d^2I}{da^2} = 2 \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos (2ax)}{x^2+1} \; dx \] Using the result from the \( \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos x}{x^2+1} \; dx \), we know that: \[ \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos (ax)}{x^2+1} \; dx = \frac{\pi}{2} e^{-a} \] Thus, \[ \frac{d^2I}{da^2} = 2 \frac{\pi}{2} e^{-2a} = \pi e^{-2a} \] Integrating both sides, we have: \[ \frac{dI}{da} = \int \pi e^{-2a} \; da + c_1 = - \frac{\pi e^{-2a}}{2} + c_1 \] Now we know from equation (2) that \( I^{\prime}(0) = 0 \). Hence substituting a = 0 in above equation: \[ I^{\prime}(0) = - \frac{\pi e^{-20}}{2} + c_1 \] \[ c_1 = \frac{\pi}{2} \] \[ \frac{dI}{da} = - \frac{\pi e^{-2a}}{2} + \frac{\pi}{2} \] Integrating both sides again: \[ I(a) = \frac{\pi e^{-2a}}{4} + \frac{\pi a}{2} + c_2 \] Now we know from equation (1) that \( I(0) = 0 \). Hence substituting a = 0 in above equation: \[ I(0) = \frac{\pi e^{-20}}{4} + 0 + c_2 \] \[ c_2 = - \frac{\pi}{4} \] Thus, finally: \[ I(a) = \frac{\pi e^{-2a}}{4} + \frac{\pi a}{2} - \frac{\pi}{4} \] The solution to the original equation is obtained when we substitute a = 1. \[ I(1) = \frac{\pi e^{-2}}{4} + \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{4} \] \[ I(1) = \frac{\pi}{4 e^2} + \frac{\pi}{4} \] Hence, \[ \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin^2 x}{x^2(x^2+1)} \; dx = \frac{\pi}{4 e^2} + \frac{\pi}{4} \]
